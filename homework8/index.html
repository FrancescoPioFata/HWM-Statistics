Fundamental Property: Pascal's Rule

C(n, k) = C(n-1, k-1) + C(n-1, k)

This recurrence relation reflects a simple combinatorial truth: to choose k items from n items, we can either include a particular item (then choose k-1 from the remaining n-1) or exclude it (then choose k from the remaining n-1).

Connection to Bernoulli Processes

Row n of Pascal's triangle contains all binomial coefficients C(n, k) for k = 0, 1, ..., n. These coefficients correspond exactly to the number of paths in a Bernoulli process that lead to k successes in n trials.

üéØ Interpretation in Security Model

If we simulate n = 6 attacks, the 6th row of Pascal's triangle (1, 6, 15, 20, 15, 6, 1) tells us:

  • 1 way to have 0 successes (all failures): score = -6
  • 6 ways to have 1 success: score = -4
  • 15 ways to have 2 successes: score = -2
  • 20 ways to have 3 successes: score = 0
  • 15 ways to have 4 successes: score = +2
  • 6 ways to have 5 successes: score = +4
  • 1 way to have 6 successes (all successes): score = +6

The histogram shape mirrors Pascal's triangle when p = 0.5 (symmetric distribution).

Symmetry Property

C(n, k) = C(n, n-k)

This symmetry reflects the fact that choosing k items to include is equivalent to choosing n-k items to exclude. In the binomial distribution with p = 0.5, this creates a symmetric bell-shaped curve centered at the mean.

Fundamental Combinatorial Identities

Identity 1: Symmetry

C(n, k) = C(n, n-k)

This identity is crucial for understanding the symmetry in binomial distributions when p = 0.5. In the security model, it means the probability of achieving score +y equals the probability of achieving score -y when attacks succeed with probability 0.5.

Identity 2: Pascal's Rule (Recurrence)

C(n, k) = C(n-1, k-1) + C(n-1, k)

This rule shows how probabilities evolve as we add more trials. In a Bernoulli process, the number of ways to achieve k successes in n trials equals the sum of ways where the nth trial is a success (requiring k-1 successes in n-1 trials) and ways where it's a failure (requiring k successes in n-1 trials).
